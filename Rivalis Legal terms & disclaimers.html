Confidentiality & Attorney-Client Privilege

Protected Communications

Duty of Confidentiality

Attorneys have a professional and ethical duty to maintain client confidentiality. This duty applies to:

  • All information relating to representation of a client
  • Information learned during representation or in contemplation of representation
  • Information from any source, whether or not confidential or privileged
  • Past, current, and prospective clients (subject to applicable rules)

Attorney-Client Privilege

Attorney-client privilege is a legal doctrine that protects confidential communications between attorney and client:

Privilege Requirements

Communications are privileged when:

  • Made between attorney and client (or their representatives)
  • Made in confidence with reasonable expectation of confidentiality
  • Made for the purpose of seeking or providing legal advice
  • Not disclosed to third parties (except as necessary for representation)

Scope of Privilege

Privilege generally protects:

  • Legal advice and opinions
  • Communications about legal strategy
  • Confidential disclosures of facts to the attorney
  • Attorney work product prepared in anticipation of litigation

Exceptions to Confidentiality

The duty of confidentiality and attorney-client privilege are subject to certain exceptions:

Mandatory Disclosure

Attorney may or must disclose confidential information when:

  • Client Consent: Client consents to disclosure (express or implied)
  • Preventing Harm: Necessary to prevent reasonably certain death or substantial bodily harm
  • Crime-Fraud Exception: Client uses or used attorney's services to commit crime or fraud
  • Legal Compliance: Required by law, court order, or professional conduct rules
  • Fee Disputes: Necessary to establish attorney's claim or defense in fee dispute
  • Malpractice Defense: Necessary to defend against allegation of wrongful conduct

Waiver of Privilege

Privilege may be waived if:

  • Client voluntarily discloses privileged communication to third party
  • Client puts legal advice at issue in litigation
  • Client fails to take reasonable steps to protect confidentiality
  • Crime-fraud exception applies

Security of Communications

Rivalis Law takes reasonable measures to protect confidentiality:

  • Email Encryption: Encrypted email available for sensitive communications
  • Secure File Transfer: Protected client portals for document sharing
  • Physical Security: Secure office facilities with restricted access
  • Electronic Security: Firewalls, encryption, access controls, and security software
  • Confidentiality Agreements: All staff bound by confidentiality obligations

Email Communication

Standard email is not completely secure. While attorney-client privilege generally applies to email communications:

  • Unencrypted email may be intercepted by third parties
  • Email sent from work accounts may be monitored by employers
  • Email to incorrect addresses may result in disclosure
  • For highly sensitive matters, encrypted email or secure portals are recommended

Third-Party Disclosure

Confidential information may be shared with third parties when necessary for representation:

  • Expert Consultants: Accountants, investigators, expert witnesses (subject to confidentiality agreements)
  • Co-Counsel: Other attorneys working on the matter
  • Support Staff: Assistants and staff necessary to provide legal services
  • Court Filings: Information included in pleadings, motions, and court submissions
  • Opposing Counsel: Information disclosed in discovery, negotiations, or settlement discussions

Client consent (express or implied) is obtained before sharing confidential information when disclosure is not necessary for representation.
